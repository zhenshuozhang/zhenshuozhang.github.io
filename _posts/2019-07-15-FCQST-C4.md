# From Classical to Quantum Shannon Theory - Chapter 4

## Chapter 4: The Noisy Quantum Theory

### 4.1 Noisy Quantum States

- Only know Bob select the state $|\psi\rangle$ with a certain probability $p_X(x)$

- description of this state
  $$
  \varepsilon\equiv\{p_X(x),|\psi_x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}
  $$

  - X is a random variable with distribution $p_X(x)$

#### 4.1.1 The density operator

- $$
  p_{J|X}(j|x)=\langle\psi_x|\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle
  $$

- by the rule of total probability
  $$
  \begin{align}
  p_J(j)&=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_{J|X}(j|x)p_X(x)\\
  &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\langle\psi_x|\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle p_X(x)
  \end{align}
  $$

- **Definition 4.1.1 (Trace)** trace $Tr\{A\}$ of a square operator $A$ acting on a Hilbert spare $\mathcal{H}$ is defined as
  $$
  Tr\{A\}\equiv\sum_i\langle i|A|i\rangle
  $$
  where $\{|i\rangle\}$ is some complete, orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{H}$

- trace operation is linear and independent of which orthonormal basis we choose
  $$
  \begin{align}
  Tr\{A\}&=\sum_i\langle i|A|i\rangle\\
  &=\sum_i\langle i|A\left(\sum_i|\phi_j\rangle\langle\phi_j|\right)|i\rangle\\
  &=\sum_{i,j}\langle i|A|\phi_j\rangle\langle\phi_j|i\rangle\\
  &=\sum_{i,j}\langle\phi_j|i\rangle\langle i|A|\phi_j\rangle\\
  &=\sum_j\langle\phi_j|\left(\sum_i|i\rangle\langle i|\right)A|\phi_j\rangle\\
  &=\sum_j\langle\phi_j|A|\phi_j\rangle
  \end{align}
  $$

- so
  $$
  \begin{align}\langle\psi_x|\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle&=\langle\psi_x|\left(\sum_i|i\rangle\langle i|\right)\Pi_j|\psi\rangle\\
  &=\sum_i\langle\psi_x|i\rangle\langle i|\Pi_j\psi_x\rangle\\
  &=\sum_i\langle i|\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|i\rangle\\
  &=Tr\{\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\}
  \end{align}
  $$

  $$
  \begin{align}
  p_J(j)&=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}Tr\{\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\}p_X(x)\\
  &=Tr\{\Pi_j\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\}
  \end{align}
  $$

- rewrite the last equation
  $$
  p_J(j)=Tr\{\Pi_j\rho\}
  $$
  introducing $\rho$ as the density operator corresponding to the ensemble $\varepsilon$

- **Definition 4.1.2 (Density Operator)** The density operator $\rho$ corresponding to an ensemble $\varepsilon\equiv\{p_X(x),|\psi_x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}$ is defined as 
  $$
  \rho\equiv \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|
  $$

- equivalently
  $$
  \rho=\mathbb{E}_X\{|\psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X|\}
  $$

- **properties of the density operator**

  - $$
    \begin{align}
    Tr\{\rho\}&=Tr\{\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\}\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)Tr\{|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\}\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\langle\psi_x|\psi_x\rangle\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{x}}p_X(x)\\
    &=1
    \end{align}
    $$

  - $$
    \begin{align}
    \rho^T&=\left(\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\right)^T\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)(|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|)^T\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\\
    &=\rho
    \end{align}
    $$

  - positive semi-definite
    $$
    \langle\varphi|\rho|\varphi\rangle\ge0\quad \forall\varphi
    $$

    - proof
      $$
      \begin{align}
      \langle\varphi|\rho|\varphi\rangle&=\langle\varphi|\left(\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\right)|\varphi\rangle\\
      &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\langle\varphi|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\varphi\rangle\\
      &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\langle\varphi|\psi_x\rangle|^2\ge0
      \end{align}
      $$

- **ensembles and the density operator**

  - every ensemble has a unique density operator
  - a density operator could correspond to many ensembles

- **density operator as the state**

  - refer to the density operator as the state of a given quantum system
  - $\mathcal{D}(\mathcal{H})$ denote the set of all density operators acting on a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$
  - **Definition 4.1.4 (Maximally Mixed State)** maximally mixed state $\pi$ is the density operator corresponding to a uniform ensemble of orthogonal states $\{\frac{1}{d},|x\rangle\}$, where $d$ is the dimensionality of the Hilbert space.

  $$
  \pi\equiv\frac{1}{d}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}|x\rangle\langle x|=\frac{I}{d}
  $$

  - convexity

    - the set of density operators acting on a given Hilbert space is a convex set
    - if $\lambda\in[0,1] $ and $\rho$ and $\sigma$ are density operators, then $\lambda\rho+(1-\lambda)\sigma$ is a density operator

  - **Definition 4.1.5 (Purity)**

    - purity $P(p)$ of a density operator $\rho$ 

    - $$
      P(p)\equiv Tr\{\rho^T\rho\}=Tr\{\rho^2\}
      $$

    - the purity of a pure state is equal to one, and the purity of a mixed state is strictly less than one

- **density operator on the Bloch Sphere**

  - the pure qubit state
    $$
    |\psi\rangle\equiv cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)|1\rangle
    $$
    has the following density operator representation
    $$
    \begin{align}|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|&=(cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)|1\rangle)(cos(\theta/2)\langle0|+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)\langle1|)\\
    &=cos^2(\theta/2)|0\rangle\langle0|+e^{-i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)|0\rangle\langle1|\\
    &\quad+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)|1\rangle\langle0|+sin^2(\theta/2)|1\rangle\langle1|
    \end{align}
    $$

  - density matrix with respect to the computational basis
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    cos^2(\theta/2)&e^{-i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)\\
    e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)&1-cos(\theta)
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$

  - Pauli matrix
    $$
    \frac{1}{2}(I+r_xX+r_yY+r_zZ)
    $$
    where $r_x=sin(\theta)cos(\varphi),r_y=sin(\theta)sin(\varphi),r_z=cos(\theta)$, which are the Cartesian coordinate representation of the angles $\theta$ and $\varphi$

    more generally, arbitrary qubit density operator when $\|\mathbf{r}\|_2\le1$
    $$
    \frac{1}{2}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1+r_z&r_x-ir_y\\
    r_x+ir_y&1-r_z
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$

#### 4.1.2 An ensemble of ensembles

- noise unitary evolution of a noisy state

  - evolution leads to a new ensemble
    $$
    \varepsilon_U\equiv\{p_X(x),U|\psi_x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}
    $$
    density operator of it
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)U|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|U^T&=U\left(\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x| \right)U^T\\
    &=U\rho U^T
    \end{align}
    $$

  - shows we can keep track of the evolution of the density operator $\rho$, rather than $\varepsilon$ 

- noiseless measurement of a noisy state

  - outcome $j$ with probability $p_J(j)=Tr\{\Pi_j\rho\}$

  - when outcome $j$
    $$
    \rho\rightarrow\frac{\Pi_j\rho\Pi_j}{p_J(j)}
    $$

  - the probability of obtaining outcome $j$ conditioned on the index $x$
    $$
    p_{J|X}(j|x)=\langle\psi_x|\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle
    $$
    the resulting state is
    $$
    \frac{\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle}{\sqrt{p_{J|X}(j|x)}}
    $$

  - new ensemble
    $$
    \varepsilon_j\equiv\left\{ p_{X|J}(x|j),\frac{\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle}{\sqrt{p_{J|X}(j|x)}} \right\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}
    $$

  - the density operator for this ensemble
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_{X|J}(x|j)\frac{\Pi_j|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\Pi_j}{p_{J|X}(j|x)}&=\Pi_j\left( \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\frac{p_{X|J}(x|j)}{p_{J|X}(j|x)}|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x| \right)\Pi_j\\
    &=\Pi_j\left( \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\frac{p_{J|X}(j|x)p_X(x)}{p_{J|X}(j|x)p_J(j)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|} \right)\Pi_j\\
    &=\frac{\Pi_j(\Sigma_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|)\Pi_j}{p_J(j)}\\
    &=\frac{\Pi_j\rho\Pi_j}{p_J(j)}
    \end{align}
    $$

#### 4.1.4 Probability theory as a special case

- pick ensemble $\{p_X(x),|x\rangle\}$ , $\{|x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}$ from an orthonormal basis for a Hilbert space of dimension $|\mathcal{X}|$. These are classical because a measurement with the projection operators can distinguish them: $\{|x\rangle\langle x|\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}$ 

- the generalization of a probability distribution to the quantum world is the density operator
  $$
  p_X(x)\leftrightarrow\rho
  $$

- probability can be encoded as a density operator that is diagonal with respect to a known orthonormal basis

- the generalization of a random variable is an observable. example:
  $$
  X\equiv \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}x|x\rangle\langle x|
  $$

  $$
  \mathbb{E}_\rho[X]=Tr\{X_\rho\}
  $$

- the same with expectation of $X$
  $$
  \begin{align}
  Tr\{X_\rho\}&=Tr\left\{ \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}x|x\rangle\langle x|\sum_{x'\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x')|x'\rangle\langle x'| \right\}\\
  &=\sum_{x,x'\in\mathcal{X}}xp_X(x')|\langle x|x'\rangle|^2\\
  &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}xp_X(x)
  \end{align}
  $$

- notation of an indicator random variable $I_A(x)$
  $$
  I_A(x)=
  \begin{cases}
  1&:&x\in A\\
  0&:&x\notin A
  \end{cases}
  $$

  $$
  \mathbb{E}[I_A(X)]=\sum_{x\in A}p_X(x)\equiv p_X(A)
  $$

- in quantum theory
  $$
  I_A(X)\equiv \sum_{x\in A}|x\rangle\langle x|
  $$

- the expectation $Tr\{I_A(X)\rho\}$ of the indicator observable $I_A(X)$ is $p_X(A)$ 

- consider 2 projection operators
  $$
  \Pi(A)\equiv\sum_{x\in A}|x\rangle\langle x|,\quad\Pi(B)\equiv\sum_{x\in B}|x\rangle\langle x|
  $$

  $$
  \Pi(A\cup B)\equiv\sum_{x\in A\cup B}|x\rangle\langle x|=\Pi(A)+\Pi(B)
  $$

  $$
  \Pi(A\cap B)=\Pi(A)\Pi(B)
  $$

  

### 4.2 Measurement in the Noisy Quantum Theory

- overall state before anything happens
  $$
  |\psi\rangle_S\otimes|0\rangle_P
  $$

- Let $\{|0\rangle_P,|1\rangle_P,...,|d-1\rangle_P\}$ be an orthonormal basis for the probe system (dimension $d$). Suppose that the system and the probe interact according to a unitary $U_{SP}$.

- measurement operators $\{|j\rangle\langle j|_P\}$ 

- the probability to obtain outcome $j$
  $$
  p_J(j)=\left( \langle\psi|_S\otimes\langle0|_PU_{SP}^T \right) (I_S\otimes|j\rangle\langle j_P|)(U_{SP}|\psi\rangle_S\otimes|0\rangle_p)
  $$

- Sthe post-measurement state upon obtaining outcome $j$
  $$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{p_J(j)}}(I_S\otimes|j\rangle\langle j|_P)(U_{SP}|\psi\rangle_S\otimes|0\rangle_P)
  $$

- expand the unitary operator $U_{SP}$ in the orthonormal basis of the probe system $P$
  $$
  U_{SP}=\sum_{j,k}M_s^{j,k}\otimes|j\rangle\langle k|_P
  $$
  where $\{M_S^{j,k}\}$ is a set of operators

- consider the following operator
  $$
  \sum_j M_S^{j,0}\otimes|j\rangle\langle0|_P
  $$
  the first column of operator-valued entries in $U_{SP}$

- from the fact that $U_{SP}^TU_{SP}=I_{SP}=I_S\otimes I_P$
  $$
  \begin{align}
  I_S\otimes |0\rangle\langle0|_P&=\left( \sum_{j'}M_{S}^{j'T}\otimes|0\rangle\langle j'|_P \right)\left( \sum_jM_S^j\otimes|j\rangle\langle0|_P \right)\\
  &=\sum_{j',j}M_S^{j'T}M_S^j\otimes|0\rangle\langle j'|j\rangle\langle0|_P\\
  &=\sum_{j}M_S^{jT}M_S^j\otimes|0\rangle\langle0|_P
  \end{align}
  $$

- implies 
  $$
  \sum_jM_S^{jT}M_S^j=I_S
  $$

#### 4.2.1 POVM formalism

- not care about the post-measurement state of a quantum measurement, only care about the probability for obtaining a particular outcome.

- **Definition 4.2.1 (POVM)** A positive operator-valued measure (POVM) is a set $\{\Lambda_j\}_j$ of operators that satisfy non-negativity and completeness:
  $$
  \forall j:\Lambda_j\ge0\quad \sum_j\Lambda_j=I
  $$

- the probability for obtaining outcome $j$
  $$
  \langle\psi|\Lambda_j|\psi\rangle
  $$

- pure state $|\psi\rangle$, probability of outcoming $j$ is
  $$
  Tr\{\Lambda_j\rho\}
  $$

### 4.3 Composite Noisy Quantum Systems

#### 4.3.1 Independent ensembles

- two independent ensembles $A$ and $B$. $\{p_X(x),|\psi_x\rangle\}$ for $A$ and $\{ p_Y(y),\phi_y\rangle \}$ for $B$

- state on $A$ is $|\psi_x\rangle$ and on $B$ is $|\phi_y\rangle$. joint state $|\psi_x\rangle\otimes|\phi_y\rangle$ 

- density operator
  $$
  \mathbb{E}_{X,Y}\{ (|\psi_X\rangle\otimes|\phi_Y\rangle)(\langle\psi_X|\otimes\langle\phi_Y|) \}
  $$
  equal to
  $$
  \mathbb{E}_{X,Y}\{ |\psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X|\otimes|\phi_Y\rangle\langle\phi_Y| \}
  $$

- write expectation as a sum over probabilities:
  $$
  \sum_{x,y}p_X(x)p_Y(y)|\psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X|\otimes|\phi_Y\rangle\langle\phi_Y|
  $$

- tensor product obeys a distributive property:
  $$
  \sum_x p_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\otimes\sum_jp_Y(y)|\phi_y\rangle\langle\phi_y|
  $$

- note as $\rho\otimes\sigma$ 

- **Definition 4.3.1 (Product State)** A density operator is a product state if it is equal to a tensor product of two or more density operators. 

#### 4.3.2 Separable states

- $A$ and $B$ corresponding ensembles are correlated in a classical way. Describe as the joint ensemble
  $$
  \{ p_X(x),|\psi_x\rangle\otimes|\phi_x\rangle \}
  $$

- the density operator of this
  $$
  \mathbb{E}\{ (|\psi_X\rangle\otimes|\phi_X\rangle)(\langle\psi_X|\otimes\langle\phi_X|) \}=\sum_xp_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|\otimes|\phi_x\rangle\langle\phi_x|
  $$

- by ignoring Bob's system, Alice's local density operator 
  $$
  \mathbb{E}\{  |\psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X|\}=\sum_xp_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|
  $$
  similarly, Bob's
  $$
  \mathbb{E}\{ |\phi_X\rangle\langle\phi_X| \}=\sum_xp_X(x)|\phi_x\rangle\langle\phi_x|
  $$

- "ensemble of ensembles". Generate a random variable $Z$, distribution $p_Z(z)$. We then generate two other ensembles, conditioned on the value of the random variable $Z$. First ensemble $\{ p_{X|Z}(x|z),|\psi_{x,z}\rangle \}$, second ensemble $\{ p_{Y|Z}(y|z),|\phi_{y,z}\rangle \}$, where $X$ and $Y$ are independent when condition on $Z$, with density operators $\rho_z$ and $\sigma_z$ .

- density operator of ensemble 
  $$
  \mathbb{E}_{X,Y,Z}\{ (|\psi_{X,Z}\rangle\otimes|\phi_{Y,Z}\rangle)(\langle\psi_{X,Z}|\otimes\langle\phi_{Y,Z}|) \}=\sum_zp_Z(z)\rho_z\otimes\sigma_z
  $$

- **Definition 4.3.2 (Separable State)** A bipartite density operator $\sigma_{AB}$ is a separable state if it can be written in the following form:
  $$
  \sigma_{AB}=\sum_xp_X(x)|\psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x|_A\otimes|\phi_x\rangle\langle\phi_x|_B
  $$
  for some probability distribution $p_X(x)$ and sets $\{ |\psi_x\rangle_A \}$ and $\{ |\phi_x\rangle_B \}$ of pure states.

- "separable" implies there is no quantum entanglement

- **Definition 4.3.3 (Entangled State)** A bipartite density operator $\rho_{AB}$ is entangled if it is not separable

#### 4.3.3 Local density operators and partial trace

- **a first example**

  - consider the entangled Bell state $|\Phi^+\rangle_{AB}$ shared on systems $A$ and $B$.

  - consider a local POVM $\{ \Lambda^j \}_j$ that Alice can perform on her system. Global measurement $\{ \Lambda^j_A\otimes I_B \}$. The probability of obtaining outcome $j$ when performing this measurement on the state $|\Phi^+\rangle_{AB}$ is
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \langle\Phi^+|_{AB}\Lambda^j_A\otimes I_B|\Phi^+\rangle_{AB}&=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k,l=0}^{1}\langle kk|_{AB}\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B|ll\rangle_{AB}\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k,l=0}^{1}\langle k|_A\Lambda^j_A|l\rangle_A\langle k|l\rangle_B\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}(\langle0|_A\Lambda^j_A|0\rangle_A+\langle1|_A\Lambda^j_A|1\rangle_A)\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}(Tr\{ \Lambda^j_A|0\rangle\langle0|_A \}+Tr\{ \Lambda^j_A|1\rangle\langle1|_A \})\\
    &=Tr\left\{ \Lambda_A^j\frac{1}{2}(|0\rangle\langle0|_A+|1\rangle\langle1|_A) \right\}\\
    &=Tr\{ \Lambda^j_A\pi_A \}
    \end{align}
    $$
    where $\pi\equiv\frac{1}{d}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}|x\rangle\langle x|=\frac{I}{d}$ 

- **partial trace** 

  - general method for determining a local density operator is to employ the partial trace operation

  - Alice and Bob share a bipartite state $\rho_{AB}$ and Alice perform a local measurement on her system $POVM\{ \Lambda_A^j \}$. 

  - overall $POVM$ on the joint system is $\{  \Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B\}$ 

  - Born rule:
    $$
    p_J(j)=Tr\{ (\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB} \}
    $$

  - take $\{ |k\rangle_A \}$ as an orthonormal basis for Alice's Hilbert space and $\{ |l\rangle_B \}$ for Bob. Then the set $\{ |k\rangle_A\otimes|l\rangle_B \}$ constitutes an orthonormal basis for the tensor product of their Hilbert spaces.
    $$
    \begin{align}
    Tr\{ (\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB} \}&=\sum_{k,l}(\langle k|_A\otimes\langle l|_B)[(\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)](|k\rangle_A\otimes|l\rangle_B)\\
    &=\sum_{k,l}\langle k|_A(I_A\otimes\langle l|_B)[(\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB}](I_A\otimes|l\rangle_B)|k\rangle_A\\
    \end{align}
    $$

- **Definition 4.3.4 (Partial Trace)** Let $X_{AB}$ be a square operator acting on a tensor product Hilbert space $\mathcal{H_A}\otimes\mathcal{H_B}$, and let $\{ |l\rangle_B \}$ be an orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{H_B}$. Then the partial trace over the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H_B}$ is defined as follows:
  $$
  Tr_B\{X_{AB}\}\equiv\sum_l(I_A\otimes\langle l|_B)X_{AB}(I_A\otimes|l\rangle_B)
  $$
  simplicity, suppress $I_A$
  $$
  Tr_B\{ X_{AB} \}\equiv\sum_l\langle l|_BX_{AB}|l\rangle_B
  $$

- define a local operator using the partial trace
  $$
  \rho_A=Tr_B\{ \rho_{AB} \}
  $$
  conclude
  $$
  p_J(j)=Tr\{ (\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB} \}=Tr\{ \Lambda_A^j\rho_A \}
  $$
