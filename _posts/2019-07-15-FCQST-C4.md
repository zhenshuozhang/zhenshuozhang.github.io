# From Classical to Quantum Shannon Theory - Chapter 4

## Chapter 4: The Noisy Quantum Theory

### 4.1 Noisy Quantum States

- Only know Bob select the state $\vert \psi\rangle$ with a certain probability $p_X(x)$

- description of this state
  $$
  \varepsilon\equiv\{p_X(x),\vert \psi_x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}
  $$

  - X is a random variable with distribution $p_X(x)$

#### 4.1.1 The density operator

- $$
  p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)=\langle\psi_x\vert \Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle
  $$

- by the rule of total probability
  $$
  \begin{align}
  p_J(j)&=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)p_X(x)\\
  &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\langle\psi_x\vert \Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle p_X(x)
  \end{align}
  $$

- **Definition 4.1.1 (Trace)** trace $Tr\{A\}$ of a square operator $A$ acting on a Hilbert spare $\mathcal{H}$ is defined as
  $$
  Tr\{A\}\equiv\sum_i\langle i\vert A\vert i\rangle
  $$
  where $\{\vert i\rangle\}$ is some complete, orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{H}$

- trace operation is linear and independent of which orthonormal basis we choose
  $$
  \begin{align}
  Tr\{A\}&=\sum_i\langle i\vert A\vert i\rangle\\
  &=\sum_i\langle i\vert A\left(\sum_i\vert \phi_j\rangle\langle\phi_j\vert \right)\vert i\rangle\\
  &=\sum_{i,j}\langle i\vert A\vert \phi_j\rangle\langle\phi_j\vert i\rangle\\
  &=\sum_{i,j}\langle\phi_j\vert i\rangle\langle i\vert A\vert \phi_j\rangle\\
  &=\sum_j\langle\phi_j\vert \left(\sum_i\vert i\rangle\langle i\vert \right)A\vert \phi_j\rangle\\
  &=\sum_j\langle\phi_j\vert A\vert \phi_j\rangle
  \end{align}
  $$

- so
  $$
  \begin{align}\langle\psi_x\vert \Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle&=\langle\psi_x\vert \left(\sum_i\vert i\rangle\langle i\vert \right)\Pi_j\vert \psi\rangle\\
  &=\sum_i\langle\psi_x\vert i\rangle\langle i\vert \Pi_j\psi_x\rangle\\
  &=\sum_i\langle i\vert \Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert i\rangle\\
  &=Tr\{\Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \}
  \end{align}
  $$

  $$
  \begin{align}
  p_J(j)&=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}Tr\{\Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \}p_X(x)\\
  &=Tr\{\Pi_j\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \}
  \end{align}
  $$

- rewrite the last equation
  $$
  p_J(j)=Tr\{\Pi_j\rho\}
  $$
  introducing $\rho$ as the density operator corresponding to the ensemble $\varepsilon$

- **Definition 4.1.2 (Density Operator)** The density operator $\rho$ corresponding to an ensemble $\varepsilon\equiv\{p_X(x),\vert \psi_x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}$ is defined as 
  $$
  \rho\equiv \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert 
  $$

- equivalently
  $$
  \rho=\mathbb{E}_X\{\vert \psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X\vert \}
  $$

- **properties of the density operator**

  - $$
    \begin{align}
    Tr\{\rho\}&=Tr\{\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \}\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)Tr\{\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \}\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\langle\psi_x\vert \psi_x\rangle\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{x}}p_X(x)\\
    &=1
    \end{align}
    $$

  - $$
    \begin{align}
    \rho^T&=\left(\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \right)^T\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)(\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert )^T\\
    &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \\
    &=\rho
    \end{align}
    $$

  - positive semi-definite
    $$
    \langle\varphi\vert \rho\vert \varphi\rangle\ge0\quad \forall\varphi
    $$

    - proof
      $$
      \begin{align}
      \langle\varphi\vert \rho\vert \varphi\rangle&=\langle\varphi\vert \left(\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \right)\vert \varphi\rangle\\
      &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\langle\varphi\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \varphi\rangle\\
      &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \langle\varphi\vert \psi_x\rangle\vert ^2\ge0
      \end{align}
      $$

- **ensembles and the density operator**

  - every ensemble has a unique density operator
  - a density operator could correspond to many ensembles

- **density operator as the state**

  - refer to the density operator as the state of a given quantum system
  - $\mathcal{D}(\mathcal{H})$ denote the set of all density operators acting on a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$
  - **Definition 4.1.4 (Maximally Mixed State)** maximally mixed state $\pi$ is the density operator corresponding to a uniform ensemble of orthogonal states $\{\frac{1}{d},\vert x\rangle\}$, where $d$ is the dimensionality of the Hilbert space.

  $$
  \pi\equiv\frac{1}{d}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert =\frac{I}{d}
  $$

  - convexity

    - the set of density operators acting on a given Hilbert space is a convex set
    - if $\lambda\in[0,1] $ and $\rho$ and $\sigma$ are density operators, then $\lambda\rho+(1-\lambda)\sigma$ is a density operator

  - **Definition 4.1.5 (Purity)**

    - purity $P(p)$ of a density operator $\rho$ 

    - $$
      P(p)\equiv Tr\{\rho^T\rho\}=Tr\{\rho^2\}
      $$

    - the purity of a pure state is equal to one, and the purity of a mixed state is strictly less than one

- **density operator on the Bloch Sphere**

  - the pure qubit state
    $$
    \vert \psi\rangle\equiv cos(\theta/2)\vert 0\rangle+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)\vert 1\rangle
    $$
    has the following density operator representation
    $$
    \begin{align}\vert \psi\rangle\langle\psi\vert &=(cos(\theta/2)\vert 0\rangle+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)\vert 1\rangle)(cos(\theta/2)\langle0\vert +e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)\langle1\vert )\\
    &=cos^2(\theta/2)\vert 0\rangle\langle0\vert +e^{-i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)\vert 0\rangle\langle1\vert \\
    &\quad+e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)\vert 1\rangle\langle0\vert +sin^2(\theta/2)\vert 1\rangle\langle1\vert 
    \end{align}
    $$

  - density matrix with respect to the computational basis
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix}
    cos^2(\theta/2)&e^{-i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)\\
    e^{i\varphi}sin(\theta/2)cos(\theta/2)&1-cos(\theta)
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$

  - Pauli matrix
    $$
    \frac{1}{2}(I+r_xX+r_yY+r_zZ)
    $$
    where $r_x=sin(\theta)cos(\varphi),r_y=sin(\theta)sin(\varphi),r_z=cos(\theta)$, which are the Cartesian coordinate representation of the angles $\theta$ and $\varphi$

    more generally, arbitrary qubit density operator when $\\vert \mathbf{r}\\vert _2\le1$
    $$
    \frac{1}{2}
    \begin{bmatrix}
    1+r_z&r_x-ir_y\\
    r_x+ir_y&1-r_z
    \end{bmatrix}
    $$

#### 4.1.2 An ensemble of ensembles

- noise unitary evolution of a noisy state

  - evolution leads to a new ensemble
    $$
    \varepsilon_U\equiv\{p_X(x),U\vert \psi_x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}
    $$
    density operator of it
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)U\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert U^T&=U\left(\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert  \right)U^T\\
    &=U\rho U^T
    \end{align}
    $$

  - shows we can keep track of the evolution of the density operator $\rho$, rather than $\varepsilon$ 

- noiseless measurement of a noisy state

  - outcome $j$ with probability $p_J(j)=Tr\{\Pi_j\rho\}$

  - when outcome $j$
    $$
    \rho\rightarrow\frac{\Pi_j\rho\Pi_j}{p_J(j)}
    $$

  - the probability of obtaining outcome $j$ conditioned on the index $x$
    $$
    p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)=\langle\psi_x\vert \Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle
    $$
    the resulting state is
    $$
    \frac{\Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle}{\sqrt{p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)}}
    $$

  - new ensemble
    $$
    \varepsilon_j\equiv\left\{ p_{X\vert J}(x\vert j),\frac{\Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle}{\sqrt{p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)}} \right\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}
    $$

  - the density operator for this ensemble
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_{X\vert J}(x\vert j)\frac{\Pi_j\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \Pi_j}{p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)}&=\Pi_j\left( \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\frac{p_{X\vert J}(x\vert j)}{p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)}\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert  \right)\Pi_j\\
    &=\Pi_j\left( \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\frac{p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)p_X(x)}{p_{J\vert X}(j\vert x)p_J(j)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert } \right)\Pi_j\\
    &=\frac{\Pi_j(\Sigma_{x\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert )\Pi_j}{p_J(j)}\\
    &=\frac{\Pi_j\rho\Pi_j}{p_J(j)}
    \end{align}
    $$

#### 4.1.4 Probability theory as a special case

- pick ensemble $\{p_X(x),\vert x\rangle\}$ , $\{\vert x\rangle\}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}$ from an orthonormal basis for a Hilbert space of dimension $\vert \mathcal{X}\vert $. These are classical because a measurement with the projection operators can distinguish them: $\{\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert \}_{x\in\mathcal{X}}$ 

- the generalization of a probability distribution to the quantum world is the density operator
  $$
  p_X(x)\leftrightarrow\rho
  $$

- probability can be encoded as a density operator that is diagonal with respect to a known orthonormal basis

- the generalization of a random variable is an observable. example:
  $$
  X\equiv \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}x\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert 
  $$

  $$
  \mathbb{E}_\rho[X]=Tr\{X_\rho\}
  $$

- the same with expectation of $X$
  $$
  \begin{align}
  Tr\{X_\rho\}&=Tr\left\{ \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}x\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert \sum_{x'\in\mathcal{X}}p_X(x')\vert x'\rangle\langle x'\vert  \right\}\\
  &=\sum_{x,x'\in\mathcal{X}}xp_X(x')\vert \langle x\vert x'\rangle\vert ^2\\
  &=\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}xp_X(x)
  \end{align}
  $$

- notation of an indicator random variable $I_A(x)$
  $$
  I_A(x)=
  \begin{cases}
  1&:&x\in A\\
  0&:&x\notin A
  \end{cases}
  $$

  $$
  \mathbb{E}[I_A(X)]=\sum_{x\in A}p_X(x)\equiv p_X(A)
  $$

- in quantum theory
  $$
  I_A(X)\equiv \sum_{x\in A}\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert 
  $$

- the expectation $Tr\{I_A(X)\rho\}$ of the indicator observable $I_A(X)$ is $p_X(A)$ 

- consider 2 projection operators
  $$
  \Pi(A)\equiv\sum_{x\in A}\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert ,\quad\Pi(B)\equiv\sum_{x\in B}\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert 
  $$

  $$
  \Pi(A\cup B)\equiv\sum_{x\in A\cup B}\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert =\Pi(A)+\Pi(B)
  $$

  $$
  \Pi(A\cap B)=\Pi(A)\Pi(B)
  $$

  

### 4.2 Measurement in the Noisy Quantum Theory

- overall state before anything happens
  $$
  \vert \psi\rangle_S\otimes\vert 0\rangle_P
  $$

- Let $\{\vert 0\rangle_P,\vert 1\rangle_P,...,\vert d-1\rangle_P\}$ be an orthonormal basis for the probe system (dimension $d$). Suppose that the system and the probe interact according to a unitary $U_{SP}$.

- measurement operators $\{\vert j\rangle\langle j\vert _P\}$ 

- the probability to obtain outcome $j$
  $$
  p_J(j)=\left( \langle\psi\vert _S\otimes\langle0\vert _PU_{SP}^T \right) (I_S\otimes\vert j\rangle\langle j_P\vert )(U_{SP}\vert \psi\rangle_S\otimes\vert 0\rangle_p)
  $$

- Sthe post-measurement state upon obtaining outcome $j$
  $$
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{p_J(j)}}(I_S\otimes\vert j\rangle\langle j\vert _P)(U_{SP}\vert \psi\rangle_S\otimes\vert 0\rangle_P)
  $$

- expand the unitary operator $U_{SP}$ in the orthonormal basis of the probe system $P$
  $$
  U_{SP}=\sum_{j,k}M_s^{j,k}\otimes\vert j\rangle\langle k\vert _P
  $$
  where $\{M_S^{j,k}\}$ is a set of operators

- consider the following operator
  $$
  \sum_j M_S^{j,0}\otimes\vert j\rangle\langle0\vert _P
  $$
  the first column of operator-valued entries in $U_{SP}$

- from the fact that $U_{SP}^TU_{SP}=I_{SP}=I_S\otimes I_P$
  $$
  \begin{align}
  I_S\otimes \vert 0\rangle\langle0\vert _P&=\left( \sum_{j'}M_{S}^{j'T}\otimes\vert 0\rangle\langle j'\vert _P \right)\left( \sum_jM_S^j\otimes\vert j\rangle\langle0\vert _P \right)\\
  &=\sum_{j',j}M_S^{j'T}M_S^j\otimes\vert 0\rangle\langle j'\vert j\rangle\langle0\vert _P\\
  &=\sum_{j}M_S^{jT}M_S^j\otimes\vert 0\rangle\langle0\vert _P
  \end{align}
  $$

- implies 
  $$
  \sum_jM_S^{jT}M_S^j=I_S
  $$

#### 4.2.1 POVM formalism

- not care about the post-measurement state of a quantum measurement, only care about the probability for obtaining a particular outcome.

- **Definition 4.2.1 (POVM)** A positive operator-valued measure (POVM) is a set $\{\Lambda_j\}_j$ of operators that satisfy non-negativity and completeness:
  $$
  \forall j:\Lambda_j\ge0\quad \sum_j\Lambda_j=I
  $$

- the probability for obtaining outcome $j$
  $$
  \langle\psi\vert \Lambda_j\vert \psi\rangle
  $$

- pure state $\vert \psi\rangle$, probability of outcoming $j$ is
  $$
  Tr\{\Lambda_j\rho\}
  $$

### 4.3 Composite Noisy Quantum Systems

#### 4.3.1 Independent ensembles

- two independent ensembles $A$ and $B$. $\{p_X(x),\vert \psi_x\rangle\}$ for $A$ and $\{ p_Y(y),\phi_y\rangle \}$ for $B$

- state on $A$ is $\vert \psi_x\rangle$ and on $B$ is $\vert \phi_y\rangle$. joint state $\vert \psi_x\rangle\otimes\vert \phi_y\rangle$ 

- density operator
  $$
  \mathbb{E}_{X,Y}\{ (\vert \psi_X\rangle\otimes\vert \phi_Y\rangle)(\langle\psi_X\vert \otimes\langle\phi_Y\vert ) \}
  $$
  equal to
  $$
  \mathbb{E}_{X,Y}\{ \vert \psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X\vert \otimes\vert \phi_Y\rangle\langle\phi_Y\vert  \}
  $$

- write expectation as a sum over probabilities:
  $$
  \sum_{x,y}p_X(x)p_Y(y)\vert \psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X\vert \otimes\vert \phi_Y\rangle\langle\phi_Y\vert 
  $$

- tensor product obeys a distributive property:
  $$
  \sum_x p_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \otimes\sum_jp_Y(y)\vert \phi_y\rangle\langle\phi_y\vert 
  $$

- note as $\rho\otimes\sigma$ 

- **Definition 4.3.1 (Product State)** A density operator is a product state if it is equal to a tensor product of two or more density operators. 

#### 4.3.2 Separable states

- $A$ and $B$ corresponding ensembles are correlated in a classical way. Describe as the joint ensemble
  $$
  \{ p_X(x),\vert \psi_x\rangle\otimes\vert \phi_x\rangle \}
  $$

- the density operator of this
  $$
  \mathbb{E}\{ (\vert \psi_X\rangle\otimes\vert \phi_X\rangle)(\langle\psi_X\vert \otimes\langle\phi_X\vert ) \}=\sum_xp_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert \otimes\vert \phi_x\rangle\langle\phi_x\vert 
  $$

- by ignoring Bob's system, Alice's local density operator 
  $$
  \mathbb{E}\{  \vert \psi_X\rangle\langle\psi_X\vert \}=\sum_xp_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert 
  $$
  similarly, Bob's
  $$
  \mathbb{E}\{ \vert \phi_X\rangle\langle\phi_X\vert  \}=\sum_xp_X(x)\vert \phi_x\rangle\langle\phi_x\vert 
  $$

- "ensemble of ensembles". Generate a random variable $Z$, distribution $p_Z(z)$. We then generate two other ensembles, conditioned on the value of the random variable $Z$. First ensemble $\{ p_{X\vert Z}(x\vert z),\vert \psi_{x,z}\rangle \}$, second ensemble $\{ p_{Y\vert Z}(y\vert z),\vert \phi_{y,z}\rangle \}$, where $X$ and $Y$ are independent when condition on $Z$, with density operators $\rho_z$ and $\sigma_z$ .

- density operator of ensemble 
  $$
  \mathbb{E}_{X,Y,Z}\{ (\vert \psi_{X,Z}\rangle\otimes\vert \phi_{Y,Z}\rangle)(\langle\psi_{X,Z}\vert \otimes\langle\phi_{Y,Z}\vert ) \}=\sum_zp_Z(z)\rho_z\otimes\sigma_z
  $$

- **Definition 4.3.2 (Separable State)** A bipartite density operator $\sigma_{AB}$ is a separable state if it can be written in the following form:
  $$
  \sigma_{AB}=\sum_xp_X(x)\vert \psi_x\rangle\langle\psi_x\vert _A\otimes\vert \phi_x\rangle\langle\phi_x\vert _B
  $$
  for some probability distribution $p_X(x)$ and sets $\{ \vert \psi_x\rangle_A \}$ and $\{ \vert \phi_x\rangle_B \}$ of pure states.

- "separable" implies there is no quantum entanglement

- **Definition 4.3.3 (Entangled State)** A bipartite density operator $\rho_{AB}$ is entangled if it is not separable

- **separable states and the CHSH game** 

#### 4.3.3 Local density operators and partial trace

- **a first example**

  - consider the entangled Bell state $\vert \Phi^+\rangle_{AB}$ shared on systems $A$ and $B$.

  - consider a local POVM $\{ \Lambda^j \}_j$ that Alice can perform on her system. Global measurement $\{ \Lambda^j_A\otimes I_B \}$. The probability of obtaining outcome $j$ when performing this measurement on the state $\vert \Phi^+\rangle_{AB}$ is
    $$
    \begin{align}
    \langle\Phi^+\vert _{AB}\Lambda^j_A\otimes I_B\vert \Phi^+\rangle_{AB}&=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k,l=0}^{1}\langle kk\vert _{AB}\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B\vert ll\rangle_{AB}\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k,l=0}^{1}\langle k\vert _A\Lambda^j_A\vert l\rangle_A\langle k\vert l\rangle_B\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}(\langle0\vert _A\Lambda^j_A\vert 0\rangle_A+\langle1\vert _A\Lambda^j_A\vert 1\rangle_A)\\
    &=\frac{1}{2}(Tr\{ \Lambda^j_A\vert 0\rangle\langle0\vert _A \}+Tr\{ \Lambda^j_A\vert 1\rangle\langle1\vert _A \})\\
    &=Tr\left\{ \Lambda_A^j\frac{1}{2}(\vert 0\rangle\langle0\vert _A+\vert 1\rangle\langle1\vert _A) \right\}\\
    &=Tr\{ \Lambda^j_A\pi_A \}
    \end{align}
    $$
    where $\pi\equiv\frac{1}{d}\sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}}\vert x\rangle\langle x\vert =\frac{I}{d}$ 

- **partial trace** 

  - general method for determining a local density operator is to employ the partial trace operation

  - Alice and Bob share a bipartite state $\rho_{AB}$ and Alice perform a local measurement on her system $POVM\{ \Lambda_A^j \}$. 

  - overall $POVM$ on the joint system is $\{  \Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B\}$ 

  - Born rule:
    $$
    p_J(j)=Tr\{ (\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB} \}
    $$

  - take $\{ \vert k\rangle_A \}$ as an orthonormal basis for Alice's Hilbert space and $\{ \vert l\rangle_B \}$ for Bob. Then the set $\{ \vert k\rangle_A\otimes\vert l\rangle_B \}$ constitutes an orthonormal basis for the tensor product of their Hilbert spaces.
    $$
    \begin{align}
    Tr\{ (\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB} \}&=\sum_{k,l}(\langle k\vert _A\otimes\langle l\vert _B)[(\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)](\vert k\rangle_A\otimes\vert l\rangle_B)\\
    &=\sum_{k,l}\langle k\vert _A(I_A\otimes\langle l\vert _B)[(\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB}](I_A\otimes\vert l\rangle_B)\vert k\rangle_A\\
    \end{align}
    $$

- **Definition 4.3.4 (Partial Trace)** Let $X_{AB}$ be a square operator acting on a tensor product Hilbert space $\mathcal{H_A}\otimes\mathcal{H_B}$, and let $\{ \vert l\rangle_B \}$ be an orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{H_B}$. Then the partial trace over the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H_B}$ is defined as follows:
  $$
  Tr_B\{X_{AB}\}\equiv\sum_l(I_A\otimes\langle l\vert _B)X_{AB}(I_A\otimes\vert l\rangle_B)
  $$
  simplicity, suppress $I_A$
  $$
  Tr_B\{ X_{AB} \}\equiv\sum_l\langle l\vert _BX_{AB}\vert l\rangle_B
  $$

- define a local operator using the partial trace
  $$
  \rho_A=Tr_B\{ \rho_{AB} \}
  $$
  conclude
  $$
  p_J(j)=Tr\{ (\Lambda_A^j\otimes I_B)\rho_{AB} \}=Tr\{ \Lambda_A^j\rho_A \}
  $$
